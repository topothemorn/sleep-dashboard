NREM Stage 1 (N1)

1-7 minutes 3-5%

Light sleep transition from wakefulness

Theta waves (4-8 Hz)

Physiological Changes:

  • Heart rate and breathing slow
  • Muscles begin to relax
  • Body temperature starts to drop
  • Easy to wake up

Functions:

  • Transition from consciousness to sleep
  • Initial body relaxation
  • Preparation for deeper sleep stages

NREM Stage 2 (N2)

10-25 minutes 50-60%

Light sleep with distinctive brain patterns

Theta waves + Sleep Spindles

Special Features:

  • Sleep spindles (11-16 Hz bursts)
  • K-complexes (sharp waves)
  • No eye movements
  • Reduced muscle activity

Functions:

  • Memory processing and consolidation
  • Metabolism regulation
  • Body maintenance processes
  • Digestive system maintenance

NREM Stage 3 (N3)

20-40 minutes 10-20%

Deep sleep - slow-wave sleep

Delta waves (0.5-4 Hz)

Physiological Changes:

  • Slowest heart rate and breathing
  • Lowest blood pressure
  • Difficult to wake up
  • Sleep inertia if awakened

Critical Functions:

  • Tissue repair and growth
  • Immune system strengthening
  • Growth hormone release
  • Brain detoxification
  • Cell regeneration

REM Sleep (Stage R)

10-60 minutes 20-25%

Rapid Eye Movement - paradoxical sleep

Beta waves (15-30 Hz)

Physiological Changes:

  • Rapid eye movements
  • Temporary muscle paralysis (atonia)
  • Increased blood pressure and heart rate
  • Variable breathing
  • Vivid dreams

Functions:

  • Memory consolidation and processing
  • Emotional regulation
  • Creative thinking and problem-solving
  • Brain development
  • Learning enhancement

Brain Wave Patterns

Gamma Waves

25-100 Hz

Amplitude: Low

States: High cognitive processing, consciousness, learning

Sleep relevance: Minimal during normal sleep

Beta Waves

12-30 Hz

Amplitude: 30-50 μV

States: Alert wakefulness, active thinking, REM sleep

Sleep relevance: Present during REM sleep and brief awakenings

Alpha Waves

8-12 Hz

Amplitude: 30-50 μV

States: Relaxed wakefulness, eyes closed, drowsiness

Sleep relevance: Disappears as sleep begins

Theta Waves

4-8 Hz

Amplitude: 50-100 μV

States: Light sleep, deep relaxation, meditation

Sleep relevance: Dominant in NREM stages 1 and 2

Delta Waves

0.5-4 Hz

Amplitude: 100-200 μV

States: Deep sleep, unconsciousness

Sleep relevance: Dominant in NREM stage 3 (deep sleep)

Healthy vs Poor Sleep Comparison

Healthy Sleep Indicators

Brain Wave Patterns

Clear, well-defined sleep spindles in stage 2
High-amplitude delta waves in stage 3
Organized alpha waves when relaxed
Proper theta wave patterns in light sleep

Sleep Architecture

Appropriate time in each stage
Smooth transitions between stages
Adequate deep sleep (15-20% of total)
Normal REM sleep (20-25% of total)

Physiological Markers

Sleep onset within 30 minutes
Few nighttime awakenings
Sleep efficiency >85%
Appropriate blood pressure dip

Poor Sleep Indicators

Brain Wave Patterns

Fragmented or absent sleep spindles
Reduced delta wave amplitude
Intrusion of beta waves during NREM
Irregular theta patterns

Sleep Architecture

Reduced deep sleep percentage
Fragmented REM sleep
Frequent stage transitions
Excessive light sleep

Physiological Markers

Sleep onset >30 minutes
Frequent awakenings (>5 per hour)
Sleep efficiency <85%
Lack of nocturnal blood pressure dip

Consequences of Poor Sleep

Immediate Effects (Hours to Days)

Cognitive Impact

  • Reduced attention and focus
  • Impaired decision-making
  • Memory problems
  • Slower reaction times
  • Microsleep episodes

Emotional Impact

  • Increased irritability
  • Mood swings
  • Anxiety symptoms
  • Reduced emotional regulation

Physical Impact

  • Fatigue and drowsiness
  • Reduced coordination
  • Weakened immune response
  • Increased appetite

Long-term Consequences (Weeks to Years)

Cardiovascular System

High Risk

Chronic sleep deprivation significantly increases cardiovascular disease risk

Health Risks:
  • Hypertension (high blood pressure)
  • Coronary heart disease
  • Heart attacks
  • Stroke
  • Heart failure
Mechanisms:
  • Chronic inflammation
  • Sympathetic nervous system activation
  • Loss of nocturnal blood pressure dip
  • Accelerated atherosclerosis

Metabolic System

High Risk

Sleep loss disrupts glucose regulation and metabolic homeostasis

Health Risks:
  • Type 2 diabetes
  • Obesity
  • Metabolic syndrome
  • Insulin resistance
Mechanisms:
  • Glucose regulation disruption
  • Hormonal imbalances
  • Increased cortisol levels
  • Appetite hormone disruption

Immune System

Medium Risk

Sleep deprivation weakens immune function and inflammatory response

Health Risks:
  • Increased infection susceptibility
  • Poor vaccine response
  • Delayed healing
  • Chronic inflammation
Mechanisms:
  • Reduced antibody production
  • Impaired cytokine regulation
  • Decreased NK cell activity
  • Disrupted immune memory formation

Neurological System

High Risk

Poor sleep accelerates cognitive decline and mental health issues

Health Risks:
  • Cognitive decline
  • Memory impairment
  • Increased dementia risk
  • Depression and anxiety
Mechanisms:
  • Reduced brain detoxification
  • Impaired memory consolidation
  • Neuroinflammation
  • Neurotransmitter imbalances

Sleep Monitoring Methods

Clinical Methods

Polysomnography (PSG)

Gold Standard

Description: Comprehensive sleep assessment in laboratory setting

Measurements:
EEG (Brain waves) EOG (Eye movements) EMG (Muscle activity) Respiratory effort Oxygen saturation
Advantages:
  • Comprehensive data collection
  • High accuracy and reliability
  • Professional interpretation
  • Definitive sleep stage scoring
Limitations:
  • Expensive ($1,000-$3,000)
  • Laboratory setting (not natural)
  • Single night data only
  • Long waiting lists

Home Sleep Tests (HST)

Limited Scope

Description: Simplified monitoring focused on sleep apnea detection

Measurements:
Airflow Respiratory effort Oxygen saturation Heart rate
Advantages:
  • Home environment (natural sleep)
  • Lower cost ($150-$500)
  • Multiple nights possible
  • Faster access
Limitations:
  • Limited parameters measured
  • No sleep staging capability
  • Potential data loss
  • Sleep apnea focus only

Consumer Device Accuracy

Best consumer devices show moderate agreement with clinical standards (κ=0.4-0.6). While useful for general sleep trends, they cannot replace clinical assessment for sleep disorders.
